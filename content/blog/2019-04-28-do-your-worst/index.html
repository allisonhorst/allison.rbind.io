Clear and engaging data visualization is a critical part of science communication, and one that I emphasize in all courses I teach at the Bren School. So in the past, I’ve stressed the importance of customizing graphs to make them the best.

Recently, I’ve realized value in adding exercises that require students to make their worst.

Why is it sometimes good to be so (intentionally) bad?

Key word: intentionally. Creating a purposefully disgusting graph can promote learning in several useful ways:

  1. Making the worst graph encourages creativity
  2. Making the worst graph requires extensive customization
  3. Making the worst graph makes us identify specific ways that a graph can be “bad”
  4. Making the worst graph eliminates the stress of producing a perfect final product (…unless, of course, it’s perfectly awful)
  5. Making the worst graph is surprisingly freeing and fun
